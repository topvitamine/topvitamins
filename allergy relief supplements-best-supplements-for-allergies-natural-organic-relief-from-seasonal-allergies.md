# Best Supplements to Combat Seasonal Allergies

Seasonal allergies are an immune response to airborne triggers such as pollen, mold spores, and grass. Common symptoms include sneezing, nasal congestion, itchy or watery eyes, and throat irritation. While pharmacological antihistamines and nasal corticosteroids are effective for many people, a growing body of evidence supports complementary approaches using dietary supplements to modulate immune responses and reduce symptom burden.

## How supplements can help

Supplements do not replace medical care for severe allergic reactions, but several nutrients and botanicals have mechanisms that may reduce allergic inflammation or support immune resilience. For example, vitamin C acts as an antioxidant and can stabilize mast cells; vitamin D influences immune regulation; and certain polyphenols and botanical extracts exhibit antihistamine-like or anti-inflammatory effects in preclinical and clinical studies.

## Key ingredients with supporting evidence

- Probiotics: Specific strains (e.g., Lactobacillus rhamnosus) have been associated with reduced incidence or severity of allergic symptoms in some trials, likely via gut-immune axis modulation.
- Vitamin D: Observational studies link adequate vitamin D status with lower allergic disease prevalence; supplementation may be beneficial when deficiency is present.
- Vitamin C: Antioxidant and mast cell–stabilizing properties can contribute to reduced histamine release.
- Quercetin: A flavonoid with antioxidant and anti-inflammatory actions; in vitro and small clinical studies suggest antihistamine-like effects.
- Butterbur (Petasites hybridus): Some randomized trials have shown efficacy comparable to antihistamines for allergic rhinitis symptoms, though product quality and presence of pyrrolizidine alkaloids require attention.
- Stinging nettle (Urtica dioica): Traditional use and preliminary data indicate potential to reduce hay fever symptoms in some individuals.
- Magnesium: Supports smooth muscle relaxation in airways and general immune function when levels are low.

## Practical guidance for use

- Timing: Begin preventive supplementation several weeks before anticipated high pollen exposure to allow physiological effects to develop.
- Dosing and formulation: Use evidence-based dosages and products tested for purity. Prefer standardized extracts for botanicals and consider certified organic options to reduce exposure to pesticides or contaminants.
- Interactions and safety: Discuss supplements with a healthcare professional, particularly if you use prescription allergy medications, have chronic conditions, or are pregnant or breastfeeding.

## Lifestyle measures that complement supplements

Non-pharmacological strategies can enhance outcomes: keep windows closed during high pollen counts, use HEPA or high-efficiency filters, launder bedding frequently, and shower after outdoor exposure. Combining targeted supplementation with these environmental controls supports a multi-layered approach to symptom reduction.
