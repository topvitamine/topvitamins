## How buffered vitamin C differs

Buffered vitamin C contains mineral ascorbates rather than pure ascorbic acid. Ascorbates are less acidic and can be gentler on the gastric mucosa, which may benefit people who experience reflux, heartburn or stomach upset with standard ascorbic acid supplements. The presence of minerals can also alter absorption kinetics; some evidence suggests ascorbate salts are well absorbed and maintain plasma vitamin C concentrations effectively when taken appropriately.

## Potential benefits and evidence-based effects

- Immune support: Vitamin C supports several immune mechanisms, including leukocyte function and epithelial barrier integrity. While supplementation cannot prevent all infections, maintaining sufficient vitamin C status supports normal immune responses.
- Antioxidant protection: As a water-soluble antioxidant, vitamin C scavenges reactive oxygen species and helps regenerate other antioxidants like vitamin E, contributing to reduced oxidative stress in tissues.
- Skin health and collagen: Vitamin C is a cofactor for enzymes that synthesize collagen, supporting skin structure and wound healing processes.

## Usage and dosing considerations

Buffered powder forms are typically dissolved in water; common starting recommendations are a teaspoon once or twice daily, adjusted based on concentration per label. Large-dose supplementation (commonly described as >1,000 mg/day) is used by some for short periods to address increased needs, but higher intakes can cause gastrointestinal symptoms or osmotic diarrhea in susceptible individuals. It is prudent to follow product labeling and consult a healthcare professional before initiating sustained high-dose regimens, especially in individuals with kidney disease or a history of kidney stones.
