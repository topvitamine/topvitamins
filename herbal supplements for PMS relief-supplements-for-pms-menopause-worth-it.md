## Introduction

PMS and menopause are distinct phases with overlapping symptoms that affect physical comfort and emotional well-being. PMS typically appears in the luteal phase and can include bloating, cramps, irritability and mood swings. Menopause — diagnosed after 12 months without a period — and the preceding perimenopause period bring hot flashes, sleep disruption, changes in libido, and increased bone-health concerns. Many people consider supplements to address these symptoms, but how effective and safe are they?

## What supplements do and what evidence shows

Supplements supply nutrients (vitamins, minerals, fatty acids) or botanicals that may be underconsumed in the diet. Evidence strength varies: some ingredients have randomized controlled trial support, while others rely on traditional use or smaller studies. For example, magnesium and vitamin B6 have plausible mechanisms for easing cramps and mood symptoms by supporting nerve and neurotransmitter function. Calcium and vitamin D are widely recommended to support bone health after menopause because estrogen decline accelerates bone loss.

Botanical options such as chasteberry (Vitex) have some trial evidence for cyclical mood and breast-tenderness improvement, whereas black cohosh and phytoestrogens (soy isoflavones, red clover) show mixed results for hot flashes. Omega-3s influence inflammatory pathways and neurotransmitter activity and may be helpful for mood and general cardiovascular support.

## Safety, quality and interactions

Supplements are not pharmaceuticals and are regulated differently; claims must meet regional rules such as EFSA standards in the EU. Risks include interactions with medications (e.g., anticoagulants, SSRIs), variable product quality, and adverse effects at high doses. ## Lifestyle and timelines

Supplements tend to work best alongside diet, exercise, adequate sleep and stress management. Dietary changes that stabilise blood sugar, increase fibre and essential fatty acids, and reduce inflammatory foods can complement supplementation. Most supplements require consistent use for 4–12 weeks before noticeable effects; some benefits accrue slowly, particularly for bone-related outcomes.

Evidence supports certain supplements for specific goals (e.g., calcium and vitamin D for bone health, magnesium and B vitamins for nervous system support), but individual response varies.
