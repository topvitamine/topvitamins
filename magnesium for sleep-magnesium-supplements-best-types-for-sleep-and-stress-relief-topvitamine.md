# Magnesium Supplements: Which Type Is Best for Sleep and Stress?

Magnesium is an essential mineral involved in hundreds of biochemical reactions in the body, and it is commonly used to support sleep and reduce stress. Many people do not meet recommended magnesium intakes from diet alone, so supplements are often considered as an adjunct to lifestyle strategies. This article reviews common magnesium forms, their potential effects on sleep and stress, and practical considerations for choosing a supplement.

## How magnesium supports sleep and stress

Magnesium modulates neurotransmitters such as GABA that promote neural inhibition and relaxation, and it can influence the hypothalamic–pituitary–adrenal (HPA) axis that governs stress responses. Clinical and mechanistic studies suggest magnesium may improve sleep quality, reduce sleep latency, and diminish subjective anxiety in some populations. Effects vary by dose, baseline magnesium status, and the specific chemical form used.

## Common magnesium types and their properties

- Magnesium glycinate: A chelate of magnesium and the amino acid glycine. It is generally well tolerated, has good bioavailability, and glycine may add calming effects, making it a common choice for sleep support.
- Magnesium citrate: Ionic form with relatively high absorption; it has an osmotic effect in the gut and can have a mild laxative action, which may be relevant for people with constipation.
- Magnesium taurate: Bound to the amino acid taurine, which has been studied for cardiovascular and nervous system effects; it may support autonomic balance linked to stress regulation.
- Magnesium L‑threonate: A form reported to cross the blood–brain barrier more effectively in some animal studies, and it has been investigated for cognitive and synaptic effects that could indirectly affect anxiety and stress.

## Choosing a form for sleep vs. stress

For sleep-focused support, magnesium glycinate is commonly recommended due to tolerability and the combined relaxant properties of glycine. If gastrointestinal transit or constipation is also a concern, magnesium citrate may be considered, though timing and dose should be adjusted to avoid nocturnal disturbances. For stress and cardiovascular considerations, magnesium taurate is a relevant option because of taurine’s modulatory roles. Magnesium L‑threonate is of interest when cognitive symptoms or memory concerns accompany stress, though human evidence is still emerging.

## Dosage, timing, and safety

Typical supplemental magnesium doses range from 100–400 mg elemental magnesium daily, depending on dietary intake and individual needs. For sleep, taking magnesium 30–60 minutes before bedtime is commonly reported to be beneficial. Start with a lower dose to assess tolerance and increase gradually as needed. Adverse effects most frequently include gastrointestinal symptoms such as diarrhea. People with kidney impairment, those on certain medications, pregnant individuals, or people with complex medical conditions should consult a healthcare professional before initiating supplementation.
