Premenstrual syndrome (PMS) affects many people in the luteal phase of the menstrual cycle, producing physical and emotional symptoms such as mood swings, irritability, bloating, breast tenderness, and cramps. While lifestyle measures and medical treatments remain primary strategies, several dietary supplements have been investigated for symptom relief. ## Understanding PMS: a multifactorial condition

PMS likely arises from interactions between hormonal fluctuations (notably progesterone and estrogen) and neurotransmitter pathways, including serotonin and GABA. Inflammation and micronutrient status can also influence symptom severity. Because PMS presents heterogeneously, interventions that target different mechanisms—nutrient repletion, anti-inflammatory effects, or neurotransmitter support—may be helpful for different symptom clusters.

## Calcium and Vitamin D

Calcium has been associated with reduced overall PMS severity in randomized and observational studies, particularly for mood and somatic symptoms. Vitamin D facilitates calcium absorption and has independent roles in immune modulation and mood regulation. Low dietary calcium and vitamin D status correlates with higher reported PMS symptoms in some cohorts. Supplementation to achieve recommended intakes may be beneficial for individuals with confirmed insufficiency, under clinical guidance.

## Magnesium

Magnesium participates in hundreds of biochemical reactions, including muscle relaxation and neurotransmitter function. Clinical trials indicate potential benefits of magnesium for menstrual cramps, bloating, and mood-related symptoms, although dosing and formulations vary across studies. Magnesium supplementation can be useful when dietary intake is low, but renal impairment and concurrent medications should be considered.

## Omega-3 fatty acids

Long-chain omega-3s (EPA and DHA) have anti-inflammatory properties and may reduce prostaglandin-mediated cramping and breast tenderness. Some trials report modest improvements in pain and mood measures with omega-3 supplementation. Quality, dose, and baseline dietary intake influence outcomes; therefore, consistent, evidence-aligned dosing is important when evaluating effects.

## Vitamin B6

Vitamin B6 supports synthesis of serotonin, dopamine, and GABA—neurotransmitters linked to mood regulation. Trials of pyridoxine for PMS symptoms show variable but often favorable effects on mood-related symptoms at commonly studied doses. Excessive intake can cause sensory neuropathy, so adherence to recommended levels and clinical advice is prudent.

## Herbal remedies

Herbal agents such as chasteberry (Vitex agnus-castus) and evening primrose oil (rich in gamma-linolenic acid) have been studied for PMS symptom reduction, particularly breast pain and irritability. Evidence quality is mixed: some randomized trials suggest benefit, while others show limited effect. Herb–drug interactions and variable product standardization necessitate medical consultation prior to use.

## Practical approach and safety

A holistic approach that combines dietary optimization, regular exercise, sleep hygiene, and targeted supplementation is most consistent with evidence-based practice. Evaluate nutrient status, review medications, and consider contraindications (pregnancy, breastfeeding, anticoagulants, etc.) before starting supplements. ## Q&A

Q: Can supplements fully cure PMS?
A: Supplements can reduce specific symptoms for some people but are unlikely to cure PMS alone; a multimodal approach is often required.

Q: Are supplements safe?
A: Many are safe when dosed appropriately, but interactions and contraindications exist; consult a healthcare professional for individualized advice.

Q: When might improvements appear?
A: Some individuals notice changes within one to three menstrual cycles of consistent supplementation, depending on the supplement and baseline status.
