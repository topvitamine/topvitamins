## Understanding the risks
Counterfeit supplements can produce no benefit or cause harm through allergic reactions, unexpected side effects, or interactions with medications. Because many counterfeit products are unregulated, their long-term effects are unknown. Consumers should treat unusually cheap products, extraordinary claims, or unclear sourcing as red flags.

## Packaging and label checks
Packaging often reveals clues about authenticity. Examine labels for legible printing, correct logos, batch numbers, and expiration dates. Misspellings, inconsistent fonts, or seals that appear tampered with suggest possible counterfeiting. Authentic products usually include manufacturer contact information and a clear ingredient declaration; if these are missing, avoid the product.

## Verify ingredient lists and health claims
Compare ingredient lists against reputable references and be skeptical of products promising rapid cures or dramatic results. Regulatory agencies such as the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) evaluate health claims in Europe; products making unsubstantiated claims should be treated cautiously. If an ingredient is unfamiliar, consult a healthcare professional or trusted database before using the supplement.

## Safe online purchasing practices
Online shopping is convenient but increases exposure to counterfeit listings. Prefer official brand sites and verified retailers, and avoid unknown third-party sellers. Check for secure payment methods, clear return policies, and consistent product photos and descriptions. Regulatory agencies and independent testing organizations play a key role in assessing supplement safety and verifying label claims. Look for products that reference third-party testing or certifications; these indicate the product has undergone additional quality checks beyond basic manufacturing controls.
