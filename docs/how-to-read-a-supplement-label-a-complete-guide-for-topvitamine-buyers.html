A clear supplement label describes what is in each capsule, tablet, or powder and provides information such as ingredient amounts, nutrient dosages, manufacturer details, and legally required warnings. Knowing how to read these elements helps you make informed, evidence-based decisions about supplementation. What is a supplement label? A supplement label is a standardized summary of a product’s contents and usage instructions. Common elements include the Supplement Facts panel, a full ingredient list, serving size, number of servings per container, manufacturer contact details, expiration date, and any cautions. These pieces together create transparency about what you are consuming and how it fits into your overall intake. Reading Supplement Facts: the basics The Supplement Facts panel is the core of the label. It lists the serving size and the exact amounts of vitamins, minerals, and other active ingredients per serving. It also shows daily value percentages (%DV), which indicate how much of the recommended daily intake a serving provides. Always check the serving size first—every amount on the panel is referenced to that serving. Understanding nutrient dosages Nutrient dosages are the quantitative measures (milligrams, micrograms, international units) of each ingredient per serving. These numbers matter because both deficiency and excess have health implications. %DV places dosages in context—50% DV for vitamin C means one serving supplies half of the recommended daily intake. Consider your diet, fortified foods, and any additional supplements to avoid exceeding safe limits. For individualized dosing, consult a qualified healthcare professional, especially if you have specific deficiencies, medical conditions, or take medications. How to choose supplements wisely Look beyond marketing language. Check for third-party testing, clear ingredient sourcing, allergen statements, and minimal fillers. Certifications such as organic or independent laboratory verification indicate higher transparency. EU supplement regulations and what they mean for you Supplements sold in the EU must comply with regulatory requirements that promote safety and accurate labeling. Rules cover ingredient limits, permitted health claims, traceability, batch numbering, and expiry dates. Clear labeling and batch identifiers support recall traceability and consumer protection. Additional practical tips Always check expiration dates and batch numbers. Be wary of exaggerated claims such as “miracle cure.” Reputable products prioritize clear, verifiable information over marketing hyperbole. Store supplements as instructed to preserve potency and consult healthcare providers about interactions and appropriate dosing.
