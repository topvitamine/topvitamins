# The Top 5 Supplements for Better Sleep Without Side Effects Good sleep supports physical repair, mood regulation, and cognitive performance. Many people seek gentle alternatives to prescription hypnotics because of next-day grogginess or dependence risks. Evidence-based natural supplements can help improve sleep onset and quality when combined with healthy sleep habits. ## How sleep supplements work Natural sleep supplements contain hormones, minerals, or plant compounds that influence relaxation, circadian timing, or nervous system activity. Unlike many prescription medications, these agents typically exert subtler effects and are used to support sleep rather than induce deep sedation. Safety considerations still apply: interactions and contraindications exist, so clinical context and professional guidance matter. ## Top natural sleep aids supported by research Below are five supplements frequently studied for sleep benefits, with typical dosing and precautions. ### Melatonin Melatonin is an endogenous hormone that helps regulate circadian rhythms. Supplemental melatonin can shorten time to sleep and help re-align sleep–wake timing in jet lag or shift-work disorder. Typical doses range from 0.5 to 5 mg taken about 30 minutes before bedtime. Use low doses initially and consult a clinician for long-term use or if taking other medications. ### Valerian root Valerian (Valeriana officinalis) is an herbal remedy with sedative and anxiolytic properties in some trials. It may reduce sleep latency and improve subjective sleep quality. Forms include capsules, tinctures, or tea; dosing commonly occurs about one hour before bed. Mild side effects such as headache or dizziness are possible. ### Magnesium Magnesium plays a role in neuromuscular function and GABAergic regulation. Supplementation—often as magnesium glycinate or citrate—can support relaxation and improve sleep in individuals with inadequate dietary intake. Evening doses of about 200–400 mg are commonly recommended; consult a healthcare provider if you have renal impairment. ### Chamomile Chamomile (Matricaria recutica) is widely used as a calming tea and has modest evidence for reducing anxiety and facilitating sleep onset. Chamomile extract is also available in supplemental form. It is generally well tolerated and may be a suitable option for those seeking a gentle, short-term approach. ### Lavender Lavender (Lavandula angustifolia) is used primarily in aromatherapy; inhalation of lavender essential oil has been associated with reduced heart rate and improved subjective sleep in some studies. Delivery methods include diffusers, pillow sprays, or standardized oral preparations in research contexts. Adverse effects are uncommon but allergic reactions can occur. ## Practical considerations Natural supplements are adjuncts, not replacements, for sleep hygiene measures such as consistent bedtimes, limiting evening screen exposure, and managing stress. Avoid routinely combining multiple sedative supplements without professional advice to reduce interaction risk.
