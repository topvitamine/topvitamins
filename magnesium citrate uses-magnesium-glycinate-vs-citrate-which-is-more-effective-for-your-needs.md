# Magnesium Glycinate vs. Citrate: Which One Is More Effective?

Magnesium supports muscle and nerve function, helps regulate heartbeat, and contributes to bone and metabolic health. When dietary intake is inadequate, supplements can help restore normal magnesium levels. Two commonly available forms are magnesium glycinate and magnesium citrate; each has distinct properties that make it more suitable for particular needs. This article reviews their differences in absorption, effects, and typical uses to inform an evidence-based choice.

## What is Magnesium Glycinate?

Magnesium glycinate is a chelated form in which magnesium is bound to the amino acid glycine. Chelation can improve stability and absorption, and glycine itself has mild calming properties. Because of its bioavailability and generally gentle effect on the digestive tract, magnesium glycinate is often recommended when the goal is to raise systemic magnesium without gastrointestinal side effects.

### Benefits and typical uses

- Higher bioavailability: Studies and clinical experience suggest chelated forms like glycinate are well absorbed, helping to correct deficiency more efficiently in some people.
- Sleep and relaxation: Glycine may support sleep architecture and relaxation, making glycinate a frequent choice for those prioritizing sleep quality or stress-related symptoms.
- Tolerability: Magnesium glycinate tends to be less likely to produce loose stools or cramping than osmotic forms.

## What is Magnesium Citrate?

Magnesium citrate combines magnesium with citric acid. It is widely available, often less expensive, and effective in raising intestinal magnesium concentrations. Because citrate is osmotic, it draws water into the bowel, which underlies both its therapeutic use and its most common side effect.

### Uses and considerations

- Digestive relief: Magnesium citrate is commonly used to relieve constipation; its laxative effect is well established and dose-dependent.
- General supplementation: It also increases systemic magnesium levels and can be appropriate for correcting deficiency, although some individuals experience digestive upset at higher doses.
- Sensitivity: People with sensitive gastrointestinal tracts may find citrate irritating if taken in large amounts.

## Comparing Glycinate and Citrate

When choosing between magnesium glycinate and citrate, consider your primary goal. If improved sleep, reduced anxiety, or maximizing absorption with minimal digestive side effects is the priority, glycinate is often preferred. If relief from constipation or an affordable option for general supplementation is needed, citrate may be more appropriate.

Both forms can raise serum magnesium, but individual absorption and response vary. Starting at a lower dose and adjusting according to symptoms and tolerance is a prudent approach. ## Conclusion

Magnesium glycinate and citrate are both useful but serve different purposes.
